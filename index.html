Social Media And Anxiety in Australian Youth

Why do you use social media?

Is it to keep in touch with friends and family? To find new videos or TV shows to watch? To fill spare time whilst waiting for your order at Subway to finally arrive? No matter the reason, there is one indisputable fact about social media that we can all agree on: it is everywhere.

Most of us have welcomed social networks into our lives without a second thought. But what impact these technologies have on our mental wellbeing? Does scrolling your feed actually make you feel better? Or does it do more harm than good?

Social Media is everywhere

Average time spent using social networks per day (by region)

Over the last 12 years, the amount of time we spend on social media has increased drastically (Statista, 2024). In the Asia-Pacific region (which includes Australia), the average usage time peaked in 2022, at 2 hours and 18 minutes per day.

When does social media become a problem?

The technologies that were meant to bring us closer together, can actually lead us to drift further apart. Research shows that how you use social media has a significant effect on whether it improves or damages your wellbeing.

How do I know if I have an unhealthy relationship with SM?

There is no universally agreed upon definition for 'unhealthy' social media use. O’Day & Heimberg (2021) define an unhealthy user as someone who spends an excessive amount of time on these apps whilst socially isolating and giving up other pleasurable activities outside of SM.

For this article, we classify an unhealthy social media user as someone who often:

  1. worries when they can't access their social media accounts
  2. struggles to relax or sleep after spending time on social networking sites

Problematic social media use and anxiety disorder symptoms

The graphs above focus on Australian teenagers that use social media problematically. They demonstrate how the symptoms of various anxiety disorders increase with more frequent SM usage. Problematic SM is strongly associated with low psychological wellbeing (Worsley, Mansfield, & Corcoran, 2018) and a number of different mental disorders, including anxiety (Weinstein, 2023).

Socially anxious users may log on to these apps to compensate for a lack of in-person support, but often engage with them in a passive way (O’Day & Heimberg, 2021). This means viewing and browsing content, without directly interacting with other people. This may lead to envy and upward social comparisons, which further exacerbates their anxiety.

In addition to screen time, the reasons why you use SM has an effect too.

It's not uncommon to be fascinated by a famous person and follow them on SM. However, when our fascination becomes compulsive (i.e. celebrity worship), it can lead to anxiety, poor body image and relationships. Similarly, Many of us often feel that other people are having rewarding experiences that we are missing out on, especially when browsing SM. This fear of missing out (FOMO) can drive us to connect with others, but it can also worsen anxiety and life satisfaction, as well as contribute to problematic social media use (Kacker & Saurav, 2020).

16-24 year olds (Gen Z) are the most likely to use social media to 'follow celebrities/influencers' and to 'avoid missing out on things' and are the most avid social media users in general. They also the highest incidence of anxiety disorders out of any age group in Australia. Whilst not confirmed, it seems likely that problematic SM behaviours, such as celebrity worship and checking your phone to avoid missing out, is contributing to the rising rates of anxiety amongst young Australians (from ABS data).

Don't scroll your life away

Should I delete social media?

Whilst it's easy to blame social media at large for your anxiety, the reality is that both mental health and SM is a complex, and affects us all differently. What we do know, for sure, is that embracing community and quality relationships is what ultimately leads to higher life satisfaction. An 85 year old ongoing study on adult development at Harvard medical school found that, during our lifetime, the connections we make and maintain will impact our wellbeing more than money, genetics, social status or anything else.

How Community and Relationships lead to better life satisfaction

Lifestyle Factors

Joining a community organisation, spending time with family and making new friends are great ways to improve your health and get more out of life. SM, if used wisely, can help us facilitate these valuable relationships.

Take care of yourself

You may have gotten this advice before, but what does self-care really mean? Unfortunately, there is no one-size-fits all solution, but a good place to start by noticing your feelings throughout the day. When do you feel invigorated? When do you feel drained? Taking small steps each day to feel more energetic and healthy can lead to significant improvements later on. Here are some ideas to get started:

  • Spend no more than 2 hours on social media each day
  • Journal to reflect on what happened in your day to day life
  • Maintain a good diet
  • Have a regular sleep schedule
  • Engage in a creative hobby (e.g. painting)
  • Volunteer or join a community group that you're interested in
  • Spend time with family and friends
  • Spend time in nature

It's Ok to not be Ok

If you're struggling, know that you're not alone. There are organisations that can help you

Headspace

Headspace has centres across Australia that provide online and in-person counselling and support services. They help with mental health, physical health, drug addictions and work or study problems

ReachOut

ReachOut is a safe, online community for young people to express themselves honestly and get advice around struggles in their life. You can also speak to a trained peer worker or browse their helpful articles

Beyond Blue

Beyond Blue provides free mental health services including online counselling, an online peer support community and mental health coaching
