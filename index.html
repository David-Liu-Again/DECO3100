Social Media And Anxiety in Australian Youth

Why do you use social media?

Is it to keep in touch with friends and family? To find new videos or TV shows to watch? To fill spare time whilst waiting for your order at Subway to finally arrive? No matter the reason, there is one indisputable fact about social media that we can all agree on: it is everywhere.

Most of us have welcomed social networks into our lives without a second thought. But what impact these technologies have on our mental wellbeing? Does scrolling your feed actually make you feel better? Or does it do more harm than good?

Social Media is everywhere

Average time spent using social networks per day (by region)

Over the last 12 years, the amount of time we spend on social media has increased drastically (Statista, 2024). In the Asia-Pacific region (which includes Australia), the average usage time peaked in 2022, at 2 hours and 18 minutes per day.

The enormity of social media (SM), especially amongst youth, is reinforced when we look at the graph below. Nearly all of Generation (Gen) Z, internet users aged 16-24, are on SM and they are the biggest adopters out of any generation. This makes Gen Z highly susceptible to both the positive and negative effects of these apps.

When does social media become a problem?

The technologies that were meant to bring us closer together, can actually lead us to drift further apart. Research shows that how you use social media has a significant effect on whether it improves or damages your wellbeing.

How do I know if I have an unhealthy relationship with SM?

Academics are still figuring out the answer, but generally speaking, if you:

  • spend an excessive amount of time on SM
  • socially isolate, that is lack social contact or support
  • have given up pleasurable activities outside of social media

then you may be engaging in problematic SM use.

Above points from O’Day & Heimberg, 2021

Problematic social media use and anxiety disorder symptoms

The graphs above are sourced from a survey of Australian independent secondary school students. Students were considered problematic social media users if they met both of the following criteria:

  • were often being worried or uncomfortable when unable to access their social media accounts
  • often found it difficult to relax or sleep after spending time on social networking sites

We see an increase in the severity of anxiety disorder symptoms amongst problematic SM users, as they spend longer and longer on these apps.

Look after yourself

https://hbr.org/2021/04/theres-no-right-way-to-do-self-care Finding time to take care of your mind and body can feel impossible. But the answer isn’t a better workout routine or sleep app. It’s changing your mindset. By challenging your assumptions about self-care, you can find an approach that works for you. Self-care can look different for different people. It could be watching your favorite TV show at the end of a long day, or turning off your phone. Only you can determine what your mind, body, and spirit need to thrive. To figure out what you need, start by simply noticing when you feel invigorated and when you feel drained. Look for patterns. You should also look out for all-or-nothing thinking, as it can cause you to put too much pressure on yourself and avoid making changes at all. From there, you can start to explore small, doable changes that work in the context of your whole life There is no one-size-fits all solution to better wellbeing, but some tips include:

  1. Spend no more than 2 hours on social media each day
  2. Journal regularly
  3. Maintaining a good diet
  4. Getting good sleep
  5. Volunteer or join a community group
  6. Connect with nature
  7. Engage in a creative hobby (e.g. painting)
